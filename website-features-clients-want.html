5 Website Features Every Small Business Client Actually Wants

Small business clients rarely ask for features by their technical name. They describe a problem — "people call and ask questions we already answer on the site" or "I don't know if the website is even working" — and it's the developer's job to translate that into the right feature. These five come up again and again across restaurants, salons, clinics, and local shops.

1. A contact method that matches how their customers actually reach out

A contact form is standard, but in many markets — Pakistan included — customers overwhelmingly prefer WhatsApp over email. A floating WhatsApp button that opens a chat with a pre-filled message removes friction that a contact form quietly adds. This single feature often does more for a small business than any other addition, because it matches an existing habit instead of asking customers to form a new one.

2. Clear, above-the-fold business information

Hours, location, and phone number should be visible without scrolling, ideally in the header or a sticky bar. A surprising number of small business sites bury this information in a footer or a separate "Contact" page, which loses customers who are checking on their phone whether a business is open right now.

3. A simple way to show proof — reviews, gallery, or before/after

Small businesses sell trust as much as they sell a service. A gallery of real work, a handful of genuine testimonials, or a before-and-after section for services like renovation or beauty work does more to convert a visitor than another paragraph of marketing copy. This doesn't need to be elaborate — even five real photos with captions outperform a generic stock-photo carousel.

4. Mobile performance that doesn't get treated as an afterthought

Most small business website traffic is mobile, often on average connections. A site that looks great on a laptop but loads slowly or breaks on a phone loses the majority of its visitors before they see anything. Compressing images, avoiding heavy unnecessary scripts, and testing on an actual mid-range phone — not just a browser's device emulator — catches problems that are easy to miss otherwise.

5. A way for the client to see the site is doing something

Business owners often ask, a few weeks after launch, "is the website even working?" Simple analytics — even a basic visitor counter or a Google Analytics dashboard shared with the client — answers that question without them needing to ask. It also gives the developer a natural, low-pressure reason to check back in and offer an update, which frequently turns into repeat work.

Why these five, specifically

None of these features are technically difficult. What makes them valuable is that they solve a problem the client already has, even when the client hasn't put it into technical words. A developer who asks "how do most of your customers currently contact you?" or "what makes someone trust your business enough to book?" before writing any code usually ends up building a site that needs far fewer revisions — because the features were chosen to match the business, not added by default.

The takeaway for pricing proposals

These five features are also useful to name explicitly in a proposal or gig description. Clients recognize their own problems in specific language — "WhatsApp contact button," "mobile-optimized," "photo gallery for your work" — far more readily than they recognize generic phrases like "modern responsive design." Being specific about what's included isn't just good practice; it's often the difference between a proposal that gets read closely and one that doesn't.
